MTN Sanitization treats every cubic foot of air and surface in your home — not just what's visible. But mold is a moisture problem first. If the underlying moisture source or sources isn't resolved, mold can return. That's why every treatment includes post-treatment education on a variety of moisture management topics, and why we connect customers with vetted partners ranging from HVAC contractors to landscaping service providers.

The treatment itself is thorough and long-lasting. Most customers don't see mold return. The longevity of treatment depends on better managing the moisture conditions that allowed mold to grow in the first place. Yes. Omaha sees extremes in every direction — extreme heat, extreme cold, extreme humidity, and extreme dry. Midwesterners tend to add humidity during dry stretches, which makes sense. But when the humid days arrive (sometimes in the middle of a dry winter), that added humidity amplifies an already humid environment. The result is some of the most challenging mold conditions in the country. Basements and top floors feel it first — but the whole home is affected.

Moisture — almost always. In Nebraska basements specifically, the most common causes are high relative humidity, poor exterior drainage not directing water away from the foundation, HVAC systems not sized or configured to control indoor moisture, and slow plumbing leaks that go undetected for months or years. Nebraska's clay-heavy soil holds water near foundations longer than sandier soils, which makes basement moisture a particularly common problem across the state.

As a general rule, mold can begin to grow when relative humidity consistently exceeds 50%. In Nebraska, that threshold can be crossed quickly — relative humidity and temperature both change fast here. We recommend keeping your home between 30–45% year-round. When it comes to mold, lower humidity is rarely the concern — higher humidity is.

The highest risk periods in Nebraska are the shoulder seasons — spring and fall — when outdoor humidity is high but it's cool enough inside that the air conditioning isn't running to manage it. Without proper HVAC management or supplemental dehumidification, indoor humidity levels can climb quickly and quietly.

A digital hygrometer with multiple sensors is an inexpensive way to monitor humidity in different areas of your home.

Mold remediation costs vary widely depending on the size of the home, the extent of the mold problem, and the method used. Traditional remediation — which involves physical removal of affected materials — can range from a few hundred dollars for a small isolated area to tens of thousands for a large-scale project. Whole-home treatment approaches like ours typically fall in a different range.

At MTN PUR, a mold inspection is typically around $500, and a portion of the inspection fee may be applied toward the cost of treatment. Whole-home treatment averages around $4,500. We believe in transparent pricing — our dedicated pricing page covers what affects cost, what's included, and how to think about the investment.

The most important thing: get an inspection first. Cost should follow what you actually need — not the other way around.

People and pets need to be out of the home during the treatment itself, which typically takes six to eight hours. You can typically return later the same day. There's no extended displacement, no torn-out walls to live around, and no weeks-long remediation project.

A mold inspection is like a health diagnosis — mold remediation is the treatment. Just as it wouldn't be wise to undergo treatment without knowing what health symptoms you're trying to address, we always recommend starting with an inspection before committing to remediation. The inspection tells us what's there, where it's coming from, and how significant it is. Some situations need immediate treatment. Others need moisture control first. A few don't need treatment at all. The inspection is what tells you which situation you're actually in.

Sometimes you can see it — dark spots on surfaces like walls, ceilings, or around windows. More often you can't — not always because it's tucked behind a wall, but often because it's present and in plain sight, just not yet at concentrations visible to the naked eye. The most common signs are a musty smell, an unexplained allergy or respiratory symptom that improves when you leave the house, or a history of moisture issues like flooding, slow leaks, or consistently high humidity. If any of those sound familiar, an inspection is the right next step. It's the only way to know what you're dealing with — and whether treatment is actually needed.
